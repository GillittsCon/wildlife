Golden-bristle Grass

Setaria sphacelata
Golden Bristle Grass - By Anno Torr

A tufted grass with narrow, blue-green leaves. Tall golden inflorescences are seen from October to May. Habitat: Grassland; wetland.

  • Seed Production: The large, golden seed heads (panicles) are a critical food source for granivorous birds, including the Long-tailed Widowbird, waxbills, and weavers.
  • An essential larval host plant for several butterfly species, primarily within the Skipper (Hesperiidae) and Brown (Satyrinae) families.
  • Bridge between terrestrial and wetland habitats. Provides essential shelter and breeding grounds for a vast array of wetland-dependent insects, which form the base of the habitat's food web. Helps to filter water of harmful chemicals.

Fishbone Cassia

Chamaecrista mimosoides
Fishbone Cassia in Minerva - by Elaine Kool

It can grow as an annual herb, a short-lived perennial, or an erect subshrub reaching heights of 0.3 to 1.5 metres. Habitat: Grassland, rocky outcrops, disturbed areas.

  • Butterfly Host Plant: It is a primary larval host (caterpillar food) for the African Grass Yellow, the Broad-bordered Grass Yellow, and the Angled Grass Yellow
  • The bright yellow flowers provide nectar and pollen for various indigenous bees and small insects, which in turn support the insectivorous bird populations found in the Mistbelt..
  • Pioneer Species: It acts as a hardy pioneer, stabilising soil in disturbed patches or along the edges of trails within the reserve, preventing erosion.

Balloon Milkweed

Gomphocarpus physocarpus
Balloon Plant - by HQ, CC via Wikimedia Commons

An upright soft shrub with pale green stems, small cream-white flowers, and curious balloon-like seed pods. Habitat: Grassland, roadsides, disturbed areas, and open sunny places.

  • Butterfly Host Plant: A food plant for African Monarch caterpillars, which feed on the leaves.
  • The flowers attract butterflies and other insects looking for nectar.
  • The inflated pods split open to release silky-tufted seeds that are carried by the wind.
African Monarch pupa can be pale pink or green - by Anno Torr

Wild Dagga

Leonotis leonurus
Wild Dagga in flower - by Buendia22, CC via Wikimedia Commons

A tall, fast-growing indigenous shrub with whorls of vivid orange tubular flowers stacked up the stems. Habitat: Grassland, rocky places, forest margins, and disturbed ground in sunny areas.

  • Bird Pollinated: The bright tubular flowers are rich in nectar and attract sunbirds.
  • Insect Value: Bees and other insects also visit the flowers, adding to pollinator activity in the reserve.
  • Butterfly Host Plant: It is a larval food plant for the Bush Bronze butterfly.
